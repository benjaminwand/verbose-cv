Teaching Philosophy

Since March 2021

Previously

When I was asked to teach philosophy to a private student in February 2021, I felt a little confused and conflicted. Although I had studied philosophy in my early twenties, in the recent years I thought the best thing one can do with it is theory of science, and otherwise philosophy is about at good and useful as contemplating whether ketchup is a smoothie. But then, I was offered money, and, being endlessly curious, I quite like being appointed to absurd-ish positions.

Practically

The student is a retired architect. We are reading Sophie's World and I prepare homework for him. So far in his life he had been used to dealing with decidable and practical subjects and introducing him to discourse is challenging, both for him and me. Here is an example: as part of the homework for Helenism I asked 'What does it take for a good life?' and since half of the chapter was about that question, I expected him to work with some of the arguments presented. Instead he came up with a four word answer that had nothing to do with the Greeks. So in the next lesson we tried to figure out what the Cynics, Stoics and Epicureans thought what it takes for a good life.
Being a theory teacher for the first time, I have to learn to give useful homework. If I do not explicitly say 'justify your reasoning', I typically get answers like 'Yes' or 'I don't like him'. (He dislikes Aristotle.) And even if that is a little frustrating, that way I probably learn more than him. I'm convinced that trying to get him used to answers longer than four words is important in order for him to be able to learn anything about philosophy at all.
To make things less frustrating, I try mixing it up with tasks in cultural studies, for instance I asked for a short presentation about the Olympic games of antiquity. The culture-things are much easier for him than the philosophical ones.

Positioning

So, what do I think now, is ketchup a smoothie? Remembering that European philosophy started with Socrates 'I know that I don't know', suddenly made it likable. I profoundly agree to the appreciation of not knowing, as itself and as a requirement for deeper understanding. If differentiating oneself from people who feel safe in their knowledge was the birth place of European intellectual history, we can continue to learn and humanity might not be lost.
Dealing with philosophy again also made me realize that I am an Empiricist in the age of computers and big data, I believe that in order to understand things we need data that is acquired in a traceable way, which is what made me feel uneasy about theories that are theory-only, like proves of God. (One might still enjoy them like a science fiction novel though.) It would be a philosophical question to ask whether humanity is able to live in peace and if yes under which conditions, but I want a scientific, psychological or sociological answer to that and not a speculation in thin air.
So, on the one hand I'm still at 'philosophy is good for theory of science' with some added trips to rationalist sci-fi land, but I also learnt that, being endlessly curious and enjoying learning together in a dialogue, I am a philosopher.

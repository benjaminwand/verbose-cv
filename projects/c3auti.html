c3auti and the QuietQube

August - December 2019

This is a draft.

Definition

Disclaimer: I’m speaking from my perspective here, but let’s keep in mind that such group activities are always a group effort.

c3auti is a volunteer organisation that cares for the needs of autistic people at events of the Chaos Computer Club.
When I first heard of it, I laughed, because: maybe a third of the people at Chaos events show autistic traits, and suddenly we need a committee for that? But it turns out we do need one.

In August 2019 fairsein recruited me for c3auti for the Chaos Communication Camp, and as I was looking for a new team anyway, I joined. It was chaotic and enjoyable, and there were organisational challenges that spurned my ambition and my lust for improving organisational things.

At Camp

During Camp I had my first experiences with helping autistics in stressful situations, which I previously had been unfamiliar with. My way of approaching this was treating them like people who got stuck on a bad trip in hypnosis, using simplified language and assuming them to have normal needs but being limited in language and rational thinking. Although I couldn’t find connections between hypnosis and autism (I searched!), I couldn’t find any, but practically that approach was successful, I got good feedback.

Other people who worked at c3auti had different approaches, this was interesting to see. We recognize it when we see it, but explaining how to care for someone well is hard.

On the management side my contributions were little, like writing meeting protocols.
Until Camp 2019, one had to be autistic to have a leading role at c3auti and I found that problematic for two reasons: I think we should not force people to out themselves and diversity in a team is always good, it is good having neurotypical people in responsible roles too. I helped ease the way into having non-autistics at c3auti.

Congress planning

When the 36th Chaos Communication Congress drew nearer, I became a team lead at c3auti. Things that were brought forward through me were doing the QuietQube, writing better documentation, organizing things early enough and changing the staffing structure.

Documentation

In short, I did my best to have good meeting protocolls and other written material, like a shift schedule, so future people who do c3auti at congress have something to start from and don't need to reinvent everyhing every year. I knew, and made public before congress, that I would't stay at c3auti forever, and so involving and teaching future activists was important.

Staffing

A troublesome aspect at camp was the team role 'coordinator', because it wasn't well defined what they have to coordinate at all, different people understood their job differently. If one would have done the sum of everyones opinion, that would have been superhuman. For Congress I ended up splitting the job in two: a management person who would moderate meetings, make sure everyone can work well and onboards new team members, and a specialist for emergencies (coordinating emergency missions).
I'd recommend that separation for events larger than 3000 people, at small events there just aren't enough qualified volunteers for both roles.

The QuietQube

Origin and conceptual thoughts

Here is a thing that bugged me at camp: on the one hand people at c3auti were encouraged to not ask for diagnosis but on the other hand the room was for autistics, therefore by using it one would out oneself. My aspiration for inclusion is high and I think that it is necessary to ask for needs and not for diseases (whether or not one thinks of autism as a disease, or a disability). Therefore, I found it important that a room that caters for the (socializing) needs of autistics should be defined or described in a way that it caters for needs and not by 'is for autistics'.

We decladed rules as following:

  • no music
  • blinking has to be limited to the most necessary, no photo flashes
  • mild light that does not dazzle
  • no booths of organizations like Digitalcourage (only booth: c3auti, with the Auti cards + information)
  • no loud political demonstrations (e.g. Extinction Rebellion)
  • no loud art (e.g. choir)
  • conversations are allowed, but (bigger) workshops and meetings are not allowed

With all the things not allowed, I should add that c3auti had workshops and meetings elsewhere.

Conclusions

and it shows how autism is perceived by the aspects where it is problematic. Even after Greta Thunberg became so popular, autism is still described by the inadequacies and not by the talents. At least that was what I saw at the Camp at c3auti: being an emotional firefighter. And I saw very talented people at c3auti, autistics are quite wonderful in their own way.
Also, for buildup and teardown of things, kanban boards are superbe.

They might not be aware of it, but neurotypical people get overwhelmed at congress too, particularly the more introverted ones. The QuietQube was supposed to be for everyone and it fit in beautifully.
We got feedback like 'Really this didn't exist before?! It is so obviously needed.' and 'Suddenly I'm not disabled anymore', which confirms my understanding of inclusion.
